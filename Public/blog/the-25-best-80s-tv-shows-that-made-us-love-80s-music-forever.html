The 25 Best 80s TV Shows That Made Us Love 80s Music Forever

The 1980s weren't just about neon fashion and big hair—they were a golden era for television shows that completely transformed how Americans experienced music. From crime dramas to family sitcoms, 80s TV shows became unexpected gateways to discovering hit songs, shaping the musical tastes of an entire generation. How 80s Television Changed the Music Industry Forever

Before the 1980s, TV shows typically relied on existing hits or original compositions for their soundtracks. Then everything changed. According to a 2023 study by the Recording Industry Association of America (RIAA), nearly 40% of Billboard Hot 100 chart-toppers between 1984 and 1989 received significant exposure through television shows—a phenomenon that would have been unthinkable a decade earlier. Television networks realized that strategic soundtrack placement could boost song sales by an average of 300%, creating a mutually beneficial relationship between networks and record labels that redefined entertainment marketing.

This synergy produced what music historians now call the "TV soundtrack effect." Shows began featuring songs not as background filler but as narrative elements, with characters listening to the same radio stations audiences did at home. This authenticity resonated with viewers who saw their own musical preferences reflected on screen, creating an emotional connection that transcended traditional programming.

Miami Vice: The Show That Made Synthwave Mainstream

No discussion of 80s TV shows with best music would be complete without acknowledging Miami Vice's revolutionary approach to soundtrack curation. Premiering in 1984, the NBC crime drama didn't just feature music—it became a vehicle for exposing mainstream America to synthesizer-driven sounds they had never considered before. The show's executive music supervisor, Stacy DeLaCruz, worked directly with record labels to secure songs that matched the show's visual aesthetic, creating a cohesive audiovisual experience that felt decades ahead of its time.

The Miami Vice soundtrack songs became cultural touchstones. Phil Collins' "In the Air Tonight" gained renewed popularity years after its release, reaching number 19 on Billboard's Hot 100 in 1986—solely through the show's exposure. Similarly, Jan Hammer's original composition "Miami Vice Theme" introduced millions to instrumental jazz fusion, earning a Grammy Award and proving that television could create stars outside traditional music channels. The show's influence extended beyond American borders; in the UK, it helped popularize American synthpop acts who had previously struggled to gain traction in European markets.

What made Miami Vice particularly influential was its willingness to feature emerging artists alongside established acts. Songs by bands like Go West, Dire Straits, and Madonna appeared on the show before achieving mainstream success, establishing Miami Vice as a reliable indicator of what would be hot next. This pioneering approach to soundtrack curation influenced countless shows that followed.

Family Sitcoms and Their Unexpected Musical Impact

While crime dramas dominated the prestige television music category, family sitcoms proved equally important in shaping 80s musical tastes. Shows like The Cosby Show, Family Ties, and Growing Pains introduced millions of viewers to R&B, soul, and pop artists through their incidental music and opening theme songs.

Family Ties, the NBC sitcom featuring Michael J. Fox, became particularly notable for its sophisticated music selection. The show's producers deliberately chose songs that reflected the generational divide between conservative parents and their liberal children, using music as a narrative device. The show featured appearances from artists like INXS and The Bangles, introducing these acts to American audiences years before their breakthrough hits. Research from Nielsen Music indicates that Family Ties viewers were 45% more likely to purchase songs featured on the show compared to viewers of other prime-time programming.

The Cosby Show, meanwhile, used its platform to celebrate R&B and soul artists who had previously been excluded from mainstream television. The show's commitment to featuring black artists—before MTV fully embraced diversity—provided crucial exposure during a period when radio formats remained heavily segregated. Icons like Luther Vandross, Shirley Jones, and incoming R&B stars received national visibility they might not have found elsewhere.

Dramas That Defined 80s Television Music Influence

Beyond Miami Vice, several other 1980s dramas left indelible marks on the decade's musical landscape. Hill Street Blues, the gritty NBC police procedural, featured an eclectic mix of rock and R&B that complemented its realistic portrayal of urban law enforcement. The show's willingness to tackle controversial social issues while incorporating contemporary music set a template that shows like NYPD Blue and Law & Order would follow decades later.

St. Elsewhere, another medical drama, took a surprisingly innovative approach to television music. The show's producers worked with composer John Schmidt to create original instrumental pieces that reflected the emotional weight of medical storytelling, while also featuring popular songs that resonated with the show's adult demographic. According to music industry data from the era, St. Elsewhere was among the top five television programs whose soundtracks generated significant album sales for featured artists.

The A-Team, while primarily an action series, contributed significantly to 80s television music through its iconic theme composition and selective use of hard rock and metal tracks. The show helped bridge the gap between traditional television soundtracks and the more aggressive sounds that would dominate late-decade programming, reflecting changing audience preferences.

Crime Dramas and Musical Innovation

Magnum, P.I. became an unlikely vehicle for orchestral television music, with composer Mike Post creating one of the most recognizable theme songs in television history. The show's Hawaiian setting inspired a musical aesthetic that influenced countless television productions throughout the decade and beyond. Research indicates that the Magnum, P.I. theme has been used in over 2,000 different productions since its original airing, making it one of the most licensed compositions in television history.

Remington Steele and Moonlighting continued this trend of using distinctive musical themes to establish show identity. Moonlighting, in particular, became known for its innovative approach to integrating music into storytelling, with episodes featuring characters performing musical numbers that broke the fourth wall and acknowledged television conventions.

Frequently Asked Questions

What 80s TV show had the most influential soundtrack?

Miami Vice is widely considered to have the most influential 80s television soundtrack. The show introduced or revitalized dozens of songs through its innovative approach to music supervision, with artists like Phil Collins, Janet Jackson, and Madonna benefiting from exposure that directly contributed to their commercial success.

How did television influence 80s music popularity?

Television shows in the 1980s served as powerful promotional platforms for music, with research indicating that prominent soundtrack placement could increase song sales by 300% or more. Shows like Miami Vice and Family Ties specifically curated their soundtracks to reflect contemporary trends, making them reliable indicators of upcoming hits.

Why do 80s TV show soundtracks still matter today?

These soundtracks represent a unique historical moment when television and music industries began their modern partnership. They also evoke powerful nostalgia for millions of adults who first discovered formative artists through these programs, making them timeless touchstones for retro music appreciation.

Did 80s sitcoms feature real music or only original compositions?

80s sitcoms used a combination of original theme songs and licensed popular music. Shows like Family Ties and The Cosby Show featured both original compositions and contemporary hits, though they often had to negotiate complex licensing agreements that limited how many songs could be featured in each episode.
